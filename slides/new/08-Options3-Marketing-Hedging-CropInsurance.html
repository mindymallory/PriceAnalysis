Chapter 8

Marketing, Hedging, and Crop Insurance

Understanding how crop insurance complements—and complicates—your marketing decisions

Key Takeaways

  1. Catastrophic Protection
    Purchasing crop insurance provides protection against catastrophic loss in the event of crop failure, and/or significant price decline between planting and harvest
  2. Variable Protection
    How much protection crop insurance alone provides varies based on pre-harvest price movements
  3. Insurance ≠ Marketing Plan
    Crop insurance alone does not provide an optimal marketing plan for some scenarios of price movement planting-harvest

A Brief History of Federal Farm Support

The history of market interventions and price supports in the United States is long and varied.

Understanding the economic and political motivations for moving from price supports and ad hoc disaster payments to federally subsidized crop insurance helps explain why the current system works the way it does.

The Core Question

Why did we move away from price supports and disaster payments to the current crop insurance system?

Problem #1: Price Supports Distort Markets

Economic Inefficiency

Price supports create inefficiency in production decisions by:

  • Artificially raising the return to production
  • Incentivizing overproduction
  • Distorting resource allocation
  • Encouraging production on marginal land

Market Distortion Mechanism

When prices are supported above market-clearing levels:

  • Quantity supplied exceeds quantity demanded
  • Government must purchase surplus
  • Storage and disposal costs accumulate
  • Deadweight loss to society

Problem #2: Trade Partner Conflicts

Price supports generate ill will with trading partners through subsidizing too much production and lowering world prices to the detriment of farmers in other countries.

Mechanism

  • U.S. overproduction floods world markets
  • Excess supply depresses global commodity prices
  • Foreign farmers face unfair competition

Consequences

  • Trade disputes and retaliatory tariffs
  • WTO complaints against U.S.
  • Diplomatic friction with allies

This was a major driver of the shift away from commodity price supports in the 1980s and 1990s.

Problem #3: Ad Hoc Disaster Payments

Ad hoc disaster payments are payments made when there is a large unexpected crop failure.

Unpredictable Timing

No one knows when disasters will strike.

  • Droughts, floods, freezes occur randomly
  • Congress must act after the fact
  • Political process is slow
  • Farmers wait months for relief

Unpredictable Size

No one knows how much will be needed.

  • Magnitude varies enormously
  • Budget planning impossible
  • Creates fiscal uncertainty
  • Political battles over funding

The Political Appetite

Both of these problems—market distortion and unpredictable costs—created an appetite among lawmakers for a farm support program whose cost was more predictable.

The Rise of Crop Insurance

Over the last 30 years crop insurance has come to be the dominant way in which federally sponsored risk management is administered to commodity producers.

However, two challenges had to be overcome for crop insurance to work:

Challenge #1

Market Failure

Crop insurance cannot be offered exclusively by the private sector

Challenge #2

Low Participation

Early programs (1930s–1980s) didn't attract sufficient farmer usage

Challenge #1: Why Private Markets Fail

Crop insurance is a product that is not possible to be offered exclusively by the private sector.

When Do Private Insurance Markets Work?

Insurance markets can exist in the private sector when:

  • Insured losses are relatively small (compared to premiums collected)
  • Losses are predictable from year to year

Crop Insurance Is Neither

Crop losses fail both conditions for a functioning private insurance market.

The Correlated Loss Problem

Losses are typically highly correlated.

How This Works

When there is a crop failure, most farmers in the affected region experience a loss and make a claim on the policy simultaneously.

  • A drought doesn't hit just one farm—it hits an entire region
  • A flood affects all farms in the watershed
  • A freeze damages crops across multiple counties

The Liquidity Problem

This makes it very hard for a private insurance company to ensure they have enough liquidity to pay for losses in the event of a widespread crop failure.

Result: Without a federal backstop to the policies, the private market could never offer crop insurance.

Challenge #2: Low Participation

The first crop insurance programs administered by the federal government from the 1930s to the 1980s did not attract sufficient usage among farmers to prevent the passage of additional ad hoc disaster payments.

Why This Is a Problem

The administration of the federal crop insurance program is costly.

If taxpayers have to pay to administer the crop insurance program AND pay for ad hoc disaster payments from time to time:

  • The program is not meeting its purpose
  • Taxpayers are paying for two solutions at once
  • This is wasteful and politically unsustainable

Policy Evolution: The 1980s

1930s–1980

Early federal crop insurance programs struggle with low voluntary participation. Farmers don't buy coverage, then demand disaster payments when crops fail.

1980

Federal Crop Insurance Act of 1980

Congress attempts to solve the participation problem by making purchase of crop insurance coverage mandatory for farmers to receive other program payments.

The Logic

If you want government farm program benefits, you must also carry crop insurance. This was meant to increase participation and reduce the need for ad hoc disaster payments.

Policy Evolution: The 1990s

1994

Crop Insurance Reform Act of 1994

The mandatory coverage requirement was removed in favor of premium subsidies.

Post-1994

Congress increased the subsidy rate for crop insurance premiums to levels where farmers voluntarily purchase enough coverage to reduce the need for frequent disaster payments.

The New Approach

Rather than forcing farmers to buy insurance (which was politically unpopular), make insurance so cheap through subsidies that farmers voluntarily choose to participate.

The Current System

62%
Average federal subsidy of crop insurance premiums

What This Means

When a farmer buys a crop insurance policy, the federal government pays approximately 62 cents of every dollar of the premium cost.

The farmer only pays the remaining 38%.

The Result

With premiums this heavily subsidized, farmers voluntarily purchase sufficient coverage that frequent ad hoc disaster payments are no longer needed.

The system is self-sustaining.

Overview of Crop Insurance Offerings

There are several types of crop insurance available, each offering different kinds of protection.

Yield-Based

  • Yield Protection
  • Area Yield Protection

Protects against production shortfalls

Revenue-Based

  • Revenue Protection
  • Area Revenue Protection
  • RP with Harvest Price Exclusion

Protects against revenue shortfalls

Individual vs. Area

Individual policies use your farm's Actual Production History (APH).

Area policies use county-level yields instead.

Yield Protection

Definition

Yield Protection guarantees against a loss of production less than a percent of Actual Production History (APH).

When Are Indemnities Paid?

Indemnities are paid when production falls below the guaranteed level (coverage % × APH).

How Are Indemnities Calculated?

Payments are based on that production year's projected price.

Yield Protection: The Projected Price

How the Projected Price is Determined

For corn and soybeans, the projected price is the average of the new crop futures settlement prices during February.

Indemnity = (Coverage % × APH − Actual Production) × Projected Price

Example

If you have an 85% coverage level, 200 bu/acre APH, produce only 140 bu/acre, and the projected price is $5.00:

Guaranteed yield: 85% × 200 = 170 bu/acre

Shortfall: 170 − 140 = 30 bu/acre

Indemnity: 30 × $5.00 = $150/acre

Area Yield Protection

How It Differs

Area Yield Protection is similar to Yield Protection, but the yield guarantee is based on county yields, not the individual producer's APH.

Advantages

  • Lower premiums (less adverse selection risk)
  • Less paperwork and verification
  • No need to document individual APH

Disadvantages

  • Your loss may not match county average
  • Basis risk if your farm differs from county
  • May not trigger when you need it

Revenue Protection

Since farmers are concerned with revenue loss, not necessarily yield loss, this type of contract gained popularity.

The Key Insight

A farmer can have adequate yield but still lose money if prices collapse. Conversely, low yields with high prices might still produce acceptable revenue.

Revenue = Price × Quantity

Both components matter to the farmer's bottom line.

Revenue Protection: The Formula

Revenue Guarantee

Revenue Protection guarantees against loss of revenue below:

APH × max(Projected Price, Harvest Price)

Projected Price

The new crop futures price in February

Set before planting decisions are made

Harvest Price

The new crop futures price at harvest

Reflects actual market conditions at delivery

Why max(Projected, Harvest)?

The "max" function provides upside price protection while maintaining downside protection.

If Prices Rise

Harvest Price > Projected Price

Your revenue guarantee increases with the market. If you have a yield shortfall, you're compensated at the higher harvest price.

If Prices Fall

Projected Price > Harvest Price

Your revenue guarantee stays at the original projected price. You're protected from the price decline.

This feature makes Revenue Protection particularly attractive in volatile markets—you benefit from price increases but are protected from price declines.

Area Revenue Protection

How It Differs

Area Revenue Protection, similar to Area Yield Protection, calculates the revenue guarantee against the county APH rather than the producer's APH.

Revenue Guarantee = County APH × max(Projected Price, Harvest Price)

Same trade-offs as Area Yield Protection: lower premiums but basis risk if your farm's performance differs from the county average.

Revenue Protection with Harvest Price Exclusion

The Difference

Revenue Protection with Harvest Price Exclusion is the same as Revenue Protection, except the policy doesn't guarantee based on the Harvest Price.

Revenue Guarantee = APH × Projected Price

(NOT max of two prices)

What This Means

If prices rise after February, your revenue guarantee does not increase.

You give up upside price protection in exchange for lower premiums.

Pre-Harvest Marketing Strategies

Paired with Crop Insurance Protection

90%
of U.S. cropland is insured in some way in the Federal Crop Insurance program

The rest of this section considers marketing strategies to lock in a forward price of insured bushels.

⚠️ Warning: Marketing Uninsured Bushels

Critical Risk

Marketing uninsured bushels is very risky in the event of a crop failure.

Example Scenario

Suppose you entered into a forward contract to deliver 1,000 bushels of corn at $5.00 per bushel, but you experienced yield losses so that you only made 500 bushels.

The Problem: You cannot meet your obligation to deliver 1,000 bushels.

In this case you will have to buy out the contract.

The Correlated Risk Problem

You will have to buy 500 bushels in the spot market to fulfill your contract.

The Bad News

Unfortunately for you, your crop failure is likely to be correlated with low production for many farmers.

So prices are likely to be HIGH when you need to buy.

The Double Whammy

  • You produced fewer bushels than expected
  • The bushels you need to buy are more expensive than expected
  • Your loss is magnified by the correlation

How Revenue Protection Enables Marketing

The Safety Net

If you have Revenue Protection crop insurance, you are guaranteed:

Coverage % × APH × max(Base Price, Harvest Price)

Why This Matters

This revenue guarantee ensures you can at least buy out your forward contract in the event of a crop failure.

The insurance proceeds provide the cash needed to cover your obligation, even when spot prices are high due to regional production shortfalls.

Key Insight #1: When Prices Fall

From a hedging perspective, the marketing decision is trivial if prices fall after the base price is set.

You're Already Hedged

You already have revenue protected at a level of:

Coverage Level × APH × Base Price

So if price falls, you are already hedged by your Revenue Protection policy.

Hedger vs. Speculator

When Prices Have Fallen

If prices have fallen below the base price, your Revenue Protection already guarantees your revenue at the base price level.

Taking additional positions is speculation.

The Critical Distinction

If you take a bullish or bearish position at this point, you are a speculator.

You're not hedging your production risk—you're betting on price direction.

Key Insight #2: When Prices Rise

It does become an interesting marketing question in the case where the price rises after the Base Price is set at the first of March.

The Exposure

The producer is exposed to price uncertainty to both the upside and downside.

The Protection

However, with Revenue Protection, revenue is guaranteed at the harvest price unless price falls below the Base Price.

The Question

When prices are above the base price, should you lock in the gain or let it ride?

Marketing Choices to Examine

Choice 1: Selling Futures

Lock in the July price of $5.35 by selling December corn futures.

  • Eliminates price risk
  • Gives up further upside
  • Protected from downside

Choice 2: Forward Contract

Contract for harvest delivery at current forward price.

  • Similar to futures hedge
  • Delivery obligation
  • Assuming zero basis for simplicity

Choice 3: No Hedge

Rely solely on Revenue Protection and sell at harvest price.

  • Maintain upside potential if prices rise further
  • Accept downside risk (though RP provides floor)
