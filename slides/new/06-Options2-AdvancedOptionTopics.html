Black-Scholes Option Pricing Model

The Black-Scholes model provides the price of a European call and the price of a European put on a non-dividend paying stock.

The options we generally focus on in this book—options on commodity futures on CME Group exchanges—are American options. Since American options have all the rights of a European option plus the right to exercise at any time, an American option cannot be less than the price of the otherwise identical European option. Nevertheless, this distinction is often small or zero, so the intuition we glean from the Black-Scholes model works for the American options we focus on.

Historical Context

We present the model without proof. The original papers by Fischer Black, Myron Scholes and Robert Merton were published in 1973. The classic text by John Hull also provides a detailed explanation.

The most basic form is for options on non-dividend paying stock. We will present this model, then show the slight modification required for options on futures contracts.

Black-Scholes Assumptions

Assumptions About Assets

  • There exists a riskless asset that returns a constant risk free rate
  • The stock price exhibits instantaneous returns that are log normal. More formally, the stock price follows a geometric Brownian motion and volatility and drift are constant. The drift term defines how much the stock price appreciates on average.

Assumptions About the Market

  • There are no arbitrage opportunities
  • There are no restrictions on borrowing and lending at the risk free rate
  • Market participants can buy or sell unlimited quantities of the stock
  • There are no transactions costs

Notation

  • \(C\) Call Option Price
  • \(P\) Put Option Price
  • \(S\) Current Stock Price
  • \(K\) Strike Price
  • \(r\) Risk free rate
  • \(\sigma\) Std. dev. of stock price returns
  • \(t\) Time to maturity (fraction of a year)
  • \(N\) Standard normal CDF
\[ d_{1} = \frac{\ln\!\left(\frac{S_{t}}{K}\right) + \left(r + \frac{\sigma^{2}}{2}\right)t}{\sigma \sqrt{t}} \]
\[ d_{2} = d_{1} - \sigma \sqrt{t} \]

Black-Scholes Formulas

The formula for a call option on a non-dividend paying stock with strike price \(K\) that has time to maturity \(t\):

\[ C = S_{t}\,N(d_{1}) - K\,e^{-rt}\,N(d_{2}) \]

The price of a similar put option is:

\[ P = N(-d_{2})\,K\,e^{-rt} - N(-d_{1})\,S_{t} \]

Black 1976: Option Pricing for Futures

Key Difference from Black-Scholes

Stock trading requires the full value of the stock be spent upfront, so \(S_{t}\) references expenditures at the current time and does not need to be discounted. Futures contracts differ—entering a futures contract does not require expenditure upfront (besides posting margin). Therefore, the price of the underlying should be discounted, bringing the future value of the expenditure related to the futures contract to the present.

Call option on futures:

\[ C = F_{t}\,e^{-rt}\,N(d_{1}) - K\,e^{-rt}\,N(d_{2}) \]

Put option on futures:

\[ P = N(-d_{2})\,K\,e^{-rt} - N(-d_{1})\,F_{t}\,e^{-rt} \]

Put-Call Parity

Put and call options of the same strike price and same expiration have to follow a specific relationship.

The Core Insight

If you know the price of a call option, the price of the put option with the same strike and expiration is determined—or there is an arbitrage opportunity.

Put-Call Parity: Numerical Example

Setup

Suppose the price of the underlying is 470 cents, and a call option with strike price of 450 cents has a premium of 25 cents. Consider underlying price ranges from 400 to 500 cents.

Call Option Profit at Expiration

From 400 to 450 cents the profit would be −25 cents (you paid the premium, option expires worthless). As the price of the underlying increases from 450 to 500 cents, the profit increases one for one.

Formula: max(0, Price − Strike) − Premium

Short Futures Profit

A short position in the underlying futures contract initiated at 470 cents. Being a short position, this makes money as the price of the underlying falls.

Formula: 470 − Price

Building the Synthetic Put

Combine the long call and the short futures positions:

Combined Profit = Call Profit + Short Futures Profit

The Result: A Synthetic Put

The combined position looks exactly like the profit at expiration of a put option with strike equal to 450 cents! This is called a synthetic put position.

So, the put option premium in this example must be 5 cents, otherwise you could buy the synthetic and sell the real put (or vice versa) and make a riskless profit.

Put-Call Parity

This relationship between the call and put price is called put-call parity.

Aside: You can also create a synthetic call option by buying a put option and buying the underlying.

Implied Volatility

Of all the variables that go into the option pricing model, the only ones we don't know beforehand are the option price and \(\sigma\) (the standard deviation of returns of the underlying). If we plug in all the numbers we know and make a good estimate of \(\sigma\), the formula gives us an estimate of the option's worth.

In Practice: Reverse the Formula

We can observe markets where options are trading and know the prices at any moment. If we take these traded prices and use them in the formula, then we can solve for \(\sigma\) and get a measure of the volatility "implied" by the prices at which the option is currently trading.

Implied volatility is normalized to units of % per annum. An implied volatility of 16% means option market participants think the underlying could move (in either direction) 16% per year. The implied volatility is always changing—in fact, that is one criticism of the Black-Scholes model, which requires constant volatility.

Key Takeaway

High implied volatility means high option prices, all else equal.

Option Prices vs. the Profit Diagram

Consider the price of a call and put option with strike price of 450 cents plotted along with the intrinsic value (what the option is worth at expiration given the underlying, not considering premium paid).

Option Price ≥ Intrinsic Value

For every value of the underlying, the call price is above the intrinsic value line. The price of an option prior to expiration must be worth at least as much as its intrinsic value, with a little extra to account for the uncertainty in price that could change the moneyness before expiration.

Greatest Difference Near the Strike

The option differs from its intrinsic value most near the strike price. This is because the option price must smooth out the kink in the intrinsic value diagram.

Option Greeks: Delta

\[ \Delta_{\text{call}} = \frac{\partial C}{\partial F} \qquad\qquad \Delta_{\text{put}} = \frac{\partial P}{\partial F} \]

Delta measures how much the value of an option changes as the underlying changes. Literally, this is the slope of the line representing the option price.

Underlying Moves Higher

  • Call more ITM → call \(\Delta\) approaches +1
  • Put more OTM → put \(\Delta\) approaches 0

Underlying Moves Lower

  • Call \(\Delta\) approaches 0
  • Put \(\Delta\) approaches −1

At the Money

Call delta = +0.5, Put delta = −0.5

Option Greeks: Gamma

\[ \gamma_{\text{call}} = \frac{\partial^2 C}{\partial F^2} \qquad\qquad \gamma_{\text{put}} = \frac{\partial^2 P}{\partial F^2} \]

Gamma tells you the curvature of the option price with respect to the underlying—how much additional \(\Delta\) you get as the underlying price increases.

Visualizing Gamma

At an underlying price of 425 the option line is pretty flat (low delta), while at 475 the option line is steep and nearly equal to a slope of 1. The curvature required to get from a flat slope to a steep slope is the \(\gamma\) of the option.

Gamma: The Speculator's Friend

Large \(\gamma\) allows a trader to buy a cheap out-of-the-money option on an underlying they expect will move. If they are right, their position enjoys convex gains. The higher the \(\gamma\), the better for the directional speculator using long option positions. However, \(\gamma\) is counter-balanced by other greeks.

Option Greeks: Theta

\[ \theta_{\text{call}} = \frac{\partial C}{\partial t} \qquad\qquad \theta_{\text{put}} = \frac{\partial P}{\partial t} \]

Options lose value as expiration nears due to reduced uncertainty about where the price of the underlying will be relative to the strike. This "time decay" is theta. Theta is how option sellers make money and theta is the enemy of long option holders. Theta is usually quoted in terms of how much the option will lose value per day, all else equal.

t = 0.5 (Six Months)

ATM Call: 19.89¢ (4.4% of underlying)

ATM Put: 28.89¢ (6.4% of underlying)

t = 0.02 (About One Week)

ATM Call: 4.06¢ (0.90% of underlying)

ATM Put: 4.42¢ (0.98% of underlying)

Theta Decay Accelerates

A week passing from 180 to 173 days causes much less value loss than a week passing from 10 to 3 days. In both cases one week passed, but the option lost a lot more value both nominally and in percentage terms in the second case.

Option Greeks: Vega

\[ \text{Vega}_{\text{call}} = \frac{\partial C}{\partial \sigma} \qquad\qquad \text{Vega}_{\text{put}} = \frac{\partial P}{\partial \sigma} \]

Vega measures sensitivity of the option value to changes in volatility.

The Direction is Clear

An increase in volatility will cause an increase in option prices, all other things equal.

Other Greeks

There are other greeks that become important if you are involved in managing a large book of options positions:

Rho

Sensitivity to interest rate changes

Vanna

Sensitivity of delta to volatility changes

Vomma

Sensitivity of vega to volatility changes

We will not cover these in detail. The Greeks Wikipedia page is a good place to start if you are interested.

How Volatility & Time-to-Maturity Impact Gamma

\(\gamma\) is the friend of the option buyer and foe of the option seller because of the convex return profile.

What Makes \(\gamma\) Large?

Short time to maturity and low volatility

Short Time to Maturity

Produces large \(\gamma\), but \(\theta\) decay is fast, which works against the option buyer.

Low Volatility

Produces large \(\gamma\), but option buyers benefit most from buying slightly OTM options prior to a significant move. Periods of low volatility make a significant move less likely.

The Bottom Line

Even though the option buyer can create countless profit scenarios over many price ranges, profitable option buying for speculation requires anticipating a significant price move to pay off.

Criticisms of the Black-Scholes Model

While the model is elegant and easy to work with, most limitations stem from unrealistic assumptions.

1. Heavy-Tailed Returns

Extreme market movements (up or down) happen more often than implied by the normal distribution. Since option prices depend on the full probability distribution of potential outcomes, a model that assumes a normal distribution will chronically under-price options.

2. Volatility Clustering & Variable Volatility

If we have a large price movement, it will likely be followed by large price movements in the near future. We cannot predict whether it will be up or down, but we can be sure it will be volatile. Volatility is positively correlated and exhibits clustering—not the constant volatility the model assumes.

Volatility Clustering in Soybean Futures

Weekly soybean futures since 2010 (top) and squared log differences (bottom)

You can clearly observe the positive correlation and volatility clustering. Once there is an event that spikes volatility, it takes a while for volatility to "calm down."

Continuous Hedging Limitation

The model requires one to continuously maintain a perfectly hedged portfolio of an option and the underlying asset. This can be impossible when prices experience "jumps" or non-continuous movements—from unexpected information hitting the market, traders pulling their standing orders, or simply routine market closures. Overnight jumps can be substantial.

Delta Hedging

One proof of the Black-Scholes formula shows that if one can continuously hedge the sale of an option contract with the underlying asset so that the position is "delta neutral," then the hedged portfolio is riskless and must earn the risk-free rate.

This implies that if you know the price of the stock and the risk free rate, then you can deduce what the price of the option should be at any time. Solving that yields the Black-Scholes equation.

Delta Neutral

Recall that \(\Delta = \frac{\partial C}{\partial F}\), or how much the price of the option changes if the price of the underlying increases by one unit. A position is "delta neutral" if options and the underlying asset are held in a proportion such that for small changes in the underlying asset's price, the value of the position is unchanged.

Delta Hedging Example

Setup

The price of the nearby corn futures is 450 cents. A trader sells 100 of the 450 strike call options trading for 11 cents.

The trader is hoping to profit from theta decay. They do not want exposure to the changing price of the futures contract.

Question

How many futures contracts should they buy/sell to make their position delta neutral?

Option price curve with delta = 0.5 at 450 and delta = 0.85 at 475

Delta Hedging: Answer

The gold "hockey stick" line represents the payoff of the call option to someone long the option. The thick black line is the Black-Scholes price at the current time. The dashed lines show the slope at 450 is 0.5 and at 475 is 0.85—these are the deltas.

The Logic

Since \(\Delta\) of the 450 strike option is 0.5, the option price increases (decreases) by 0.5 times the futures price increase (decrease). Selling 100 of the 450 strike call options is like being short 50 of the futures—with a delta of 0.5, every 1 cent change in the underlying results in a 0.5 cent change in the option.

Solution

The trader should BUY 50 futures contracts at the same time they sell the call options. Now, for small changes in the futures price around 450, they are fully hedged.

Delta-Hedged Position: Small Price Move (450 → 452)

Short Calls Long Futures
t = 0
Price 11.06 cents per contract 450
Quantity 100 contracts 50
Value (11.06/100)×100×5000 = $55,300 (450/100)×50×5000 = $1,125,000 (notional)
t = 1
Price 12.18 cents per contract 452
Quantity 100 50
Value (12.18/100)×100×5000 = $60,900 (452/100)×50×5000 = $1,130,000 (notional)
Profit/Loss 55,300 − 60,900 = −$5,600 1,130,000 − 1,125,000 = +$5,000
Net P/L −$600

The trader loses $600 from this price movement. They are short the premium of $55,000 that they are hoping to pocket as time eats away at the premium. This loss will likely be outweighed by the premium decay if the price does not continue to rise too fast.

Delta Hedging: What If Prices Jump?

Scenario

Suppose now that the futures price experiences a big increase to 475 from close of the day session to open of the night session. The trader could not adjust their hedge gradually—the price "jumped" to 475.

Question 1

Is the trader's position still delta neutral?

Question 2

What should they do to get their position delta neutral?

Big Price Jump: Answer

Our trader is short 100 of the 450 call options and bought 50 futures contracts at 450. Then the price jumped to 475.

Option Position Delta

At 475, option delta = 0.85

Short 100 options: −0.85 × 100 = −85

Futures Position Delta

Futures always have delta = 1

Long 50 futures: +1 × 50 = +50

No Longer Delta Neutral

Net delta = −85 + 50 = −35. The trader needs to buy an additional 35 futures contracts to get delta neutral (+1 × 85 from futures). But doing so does not erase the loss they experienced from the rise in price from 450 to 475—it just gets them delta-hedged against small price movements going forward.

Delta-Hedged Position: Big Price Move (450 → 475)

Short Calls Long Futures
t = 0
Price 11.06 cents per contract 450
Quantity 100 contracts 50
Value (11.06/100)×100×5000 = $55,300 (450/100)×50×5000 = $1,125,000 (notional)
t = 1
Price 28.86 cents per contract 475
Quantity 100 50
Value (28.86/100)×100×5000 = $144,300 (475/100)×50×5000 = $1,187,500 (notional)
Profit/Loss 55,300 − 144,300 = −$89,000 1,187,500 − 1,125,000 = +$62,500
Net P/L 62,500 − 89,000 = −$26,000

The trader's position has a bigger loss on the short call position than gain in the long futures position, due to the significant change in slope (or delta) of the option price.

Why Is Delta-Hedging Not Fool-Proof?

Delta hedging only works if traders can continuously re-balance their option and futures positions to keep delta neutral as prices change. There are two major problems:

1. Re-Balancing Is Costly

Traders face trading costs from commissions, fees, and/or the bid-ask spread. Re-balancing too often would quickly eat into whatever profit they hoped to earn from theta-decay.

2. Prices Move in "Jumps"

Whether from information hitting the market that causes resting orders to be canceled and resent at much higher (or lower) prices, or from prices changing significantly from market close to open, the result is the same. They will have to re-balance after a large discrete jump. If the price jump goes against them, they will lock in a loss.

In our example, if the move from 450 to 475 happens between market close and the next open, the trader's position will have a bigger loss on the short call than gain on the long futures, due to the significant change in delta of the option price.

Common Theme

A fund engages in selling volatility (selling calls, selling puts, selling VIX futures, etc.) with high leverage. These funds make spectacular returns for as long as there is not a "volatility event." One day, the volatility event arrives and anyone with a highly leveraged short volatility position is highly likely to blow up—total loss of trading capital.
